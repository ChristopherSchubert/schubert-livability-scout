It starts with two facts: Slovenia, and roughly ten days in mid-May. That's all a trip needs to exist — no cities, no hotels, no commitments.

The trip opens as an empty skeleton: a stretchable window of days. Both edges are soft — May 15–25 is a guess, not a promise.

Three cities from the scout's list, dropped in rough order. The split is a default — 2 · 4 · 4 nights — nobody has decided anything yet.

Zhuzhing: grab the boundary between two cities and slide it — Bled takes a night from Piran. Zero-sum between neighbors; the trip's total doesn't move.

The flights get booked — and the trip's outer edges cure. The window can't stretch anymore without real money moving. Inside, everything is still clay.

Now the Stay shelf: five Ljubljana candidates side by side. One gets dropped onto the city — it becomes a bar spanning the leg's nights. Still cancellable, still clay.

Stays aren't 1:1 with cities. Bled's last night goes to Hiša Franko (the Soča valley splurge), Piran's last to Brdo near the airport. Drag a bar's edge — the neighbor takes the night. This is the zhuzh.

Booked stays get a 🔒 and resist the zhuzh: moving one surfaces its cancellation terms first. Union is free to cancel until May 12 — that date now matters everywhere.

Under each city, a bucket. Activities pile in from the pool — no days, no times, just wants. The bucket watches itself: ~18 hours gathered for ~3 open days — enough to lay out.

Janice already knows some answers: balloon = Tuesday. Drag it from the bucket straight onto the day — even now, before any layout. It lands pinned. The machine will never move it.

And some days are for nothing. May 22 in Piran gets marked keep it light — the layout engine must leave it nearly empty. Recovery is a feature, not a gap.

“Lay out Bled.” The machine deals the bucket across the days: booked dates pin themselves, one heavy thing per day, closed-Mondays respected, load balanced. What doesn't fit stays behind — as alternates, not casualties.

Janice drags the toboggan to Tuesday. The moment a human edits, auto-layout pauses — it proposes nothing more until she asks again. Her arrangement wins by default.

Tuesday looks right — “Solve this day.” The machine sequences by geography, inserts the drives, drops lunch in the window, surfaces the free gap, and assigns the clock. Hatched rows are its work.

She nudges lunch to 12:30 — the entry pins itself and the day says “edited since solve.” One tap re-solves around her pins. Human and machine take turns; the human always outranks.

She tries squeezing the zipline into Tuesday anyway. The machine reflows everything — and refuses to lie: it names what can't fit instead of silently cramming. One tap offers another day.

The booking spine kept itself: every to-book item with its lead time, every cancel-by counting down, cash computed (on-site ∧ cash-only = €927), the passes remembered. Nothing hand-kept.

And then the moment this whole tool exists for: the grid prints itself. Eleven days, 79 entries, every code and clothing note where it belongs — assembled from the plan, not typed into cells.

Day-of, the phone lands on today: next-up leads with the two taps that matter, the confirmation code is one thumb away, finished things fade. The plan finally pays rent.

The real story: they cancelled Piran betting on Trieste — and regretted it. Variations would let both futures live: two skeletons for the same 3 days, each holding its own refundable bookings, with a decide-by date inherited from the cancellation deadlines. Hop between them until one wins.
